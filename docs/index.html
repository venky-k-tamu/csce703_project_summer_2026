What is Post-Quantum Cryptography?
Background on the NIST standards this repository implements

Nearly all modern digital security — banking, communication, identity, data sharing — depends on public-key cryptography. Today that means algorithms like RSA and elliptic-curve cryptography (ECC), whose security rests on math problems (factoring large integers, computing discrete logarithms) that are practically impossible for classical computers to solve without the private key.

A sufficiently capable quantum computer changes that: Shor's algorithm solves both problems efficiently, which would render RSA and ECC insecure. Large-scale quantum computers capable of this don't exist yet, but the threat isn't purely future-dated — under a “harvest now, decrypt later” attack, an adversary can record encrypted traffic today and simply wait until quantum hardware catches up to decrypt it. Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) refers to algorithms designed to resist both classical and quantum attack.

In August 2024 the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) finalized the first three PQC standards. Two — ML-KEM and ML-DSA — are built on lattice cryptography, where security reduces to the hardness of Module-LWE (Learning With Errors) and Module-SIS (Short Integer Solution) problems over high-dimensional structured lattices. The third, SLH-DSA, takes a different approach entirely, relying only on the one-wayness of a cryptographic hash function (WOTS+ chains, Merkle trees, and a hypertree of one-time signatures) — a conservative fallback that doesn't depend on lattice assumptions at all.
